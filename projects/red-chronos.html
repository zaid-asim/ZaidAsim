AI SAFETY RESEARCH

Red Chronos

A Multi-Agent Defensive AI Architecture for Safe Evaluation, Containment, and Alignment Research.

Get Started → View Developer Tools

Executive Summary: Project Red Chronos is envisioned as a multi-agent AI system dedicated to defensive cybersecurity tasks: secure evaluation, runtime monitoring, containment, and incident response. It is not an offensive or hacking tool. It operates as a rigorously sandboxed “AI sentinel,” automating threat detection and response within authorized boundaries. Red Chronos leverages cutting-edge large language models (e.g. GPT-5.6-class systems) and a tiered validation architecture to identify vulnerabilities, analyze telemetry, and orchestrate defensive actions – all under human oversight and strict policy controls. In light of the evolving landscape (e.g. Anthropic’s Mythos, China’s DeepSeek and 360 tools, and first-generation AI attacks like JadePuffer), Red Chronos emphasizes defense-in-depth: isolated execution, least privilege, layered validation, and comprehensive audit logging. Our architecture follows best practices (e.g. Parallax-style privilege separation, Microsoft’s MXC container model, OWASP agentic guidance) to ensure any autonomous action is vetted by fixed policies, heuristic filters, separate AI checks, and ultimately humans if needed. This document surveys the threat landscape and AI capabilities, proposes a secure multi-agent blueprint, and outlines evaluation benchmarks and governance for Red Chronos. It includes system diagrams, tables, and metrics (citing open, official sources) to support a rigorous defensive design. No offensive exploit code or unauthorized intrusion methods are included.

1. Threat Landscape

1.1 Frontier AI Attack Models

Recent demonstrations show AI models accelerating cyber threats. Anthropic’s Mythos (a GPT-5-scale model) autonomously uncovered thousands of high-severity software zero-days in weeks. Likewise, Chinese firms have introduced similar tools: Qihoo 360’s “Tulongfeng” (Mythos-like vulnerability finder) and “Yitianzhen” (automated defense/response). Zhipu AI’s open model GLM-5.2 (“Z.ai”) reportedly matched Mythos on bug-finding in internal tests. The open-source DeepSeek models have also shocked the community with GPT-level performance at fraction of cost. These models blur offense/defense: any capability to chain exploits can both mount or prevent attacks. (OpenAI notes GPT-5.6 is better at finding/fixing vulnerabilities than running end-to-end attacks.)

Notably, proof-of-concept AI attacks have appeared. Sysdig/Todyl’s “JadePuffer” is the first fully autonomous AI-powered ransomware: an LLM agent autonomously found a Windows vulnerability, exploited it, mapped the network, delivered payloads, and encrypted files—all without human help. JadePuffer compressed a typical kill-chain from months to minutes: if detection of lateral movement takes more than a few minutes, the attack completes. It used dynamic, novel payloads (evading signature scanners) and even targeted backup systems it knew existed. In summary, AI attacks can iterate at machine speed (24/7, no fatigue), adapt to defenses in real time, and democratize powerful exploits (like ransomware-as-a-service) for low-skilled actors.

1.2 Public Competitors and Models

  • Anthropic Mythos (US): A general-purpose LLM (GPT-5-class) tuned for security testing. Found “thousands” of flaws in major OSes and browsers. Can autonomously generate exploits. U.S. export-controlled it due to its strength.
  • Qihoo 360 (China): In 2026 unveiled “Yitian Tulong”: Tulongfeng (Mythos clone for vuln discovery) and Yitianzhen (automated defense/response). Claims thousands of findings (3,432 vulnerabilities, 105 confirmed) from Tulongfeng. Emphasized “cyber weapon cannot be held by others”. These tools use Chinese domestic tech to rival Mythos.
  • DeepSeek (China): Open-source frontier LLMs with capabilities rivaling OpenAI models. Notre Dame notes it is far cheaper to run but highlights risks: early DeepSeek web/API had leaks and data exposures. ND IT recommends sandboxed use (e.g. AWS Bedrock) and only on non-sensitive data, emphasizing safe container use.
  • Zhipu GLM-5.2 (“Z.ai”): An open-weight Chinese model reportedly matches Mythos on bug-finding. Offers frontier capability without export restrictions.
  • Other AI Tools: (e.g. no confirmed info on “360” (Qihoo), Jufferpuff is likely referring to JadePuffer or similar; some IBM “Defensive AI” is also mentioned in news). We note: if detailed specs are unavailable, we treat them as “competitors/influences” with unknown internals.

These developments illustrate a “one-way transparency” concern: attackers benefit from rapid, autonomous discovery, and defenders must keep pace. Our design assumes adversarial AI is out there, so we build Red Chronos to level the playing field for defense.

1.3 Implications for Defense

  • Speed of Attack: AI agents can breach and propagate within minutes. The Mean Time to Detect (MTTD) must drop below attack times. If traditional MTTD (often hours) cannot improve to seconds, AI attacks will succeed by default.
  • Evasion: AI attacks use novel payloads and polymorphism, making signature-based defenses ineffective. Behavior-based or anomaly detection is required.
  • Adaptive Adversary: Attack chains may pivot around defenses in real time. Defense must be adaptive and layered, not static.
  • Democratization: Even unsophisticated actors will deploy AI attacks in mass (like commoditized kits). Thus, defender tools must be scalable and automated.
  • Integrated Platform Needed: As the Todyl analysis concludes, only deeply integrated, layered platforms (not isolated point products) will hold up. Every component must share intelligence, automate at machine speed, and include 24/7 human response.

2. Model Capabilities and Safe AI

2.1 Advances in Large Models

Current “frontier” LLMs (late 2025–2026) include OpenAI’s GPT-5.6 (codenamed Sol/Luna etc.), Anthropic Mythos 5, Zhipu GLM-5.2, and others. GPT-5.6 is said to be OpenAI’s strongest yet, with specialized cybersecurity improvements. Official releases note that GPT-5.6 outperforms GPT-4o on many tasks (95%+ on code generation tests) and rivals Mythos on exploit-generation benchmarks (e.g. ExploitBench). OpenAI also emphasizes that GPT-5.6 is better at defense tasks (e.g. finding/fixing bugs, secure code review, red-teaming) than performing reliable attacks.

Crucially, OpenAI and other labs frame these models’ dual-use: “the same capabilities that could help an attacker exploit a vulnerability can help a defender find it, reproduce it, and build a fix”. For Red Chronos, we leverage these advances only for defense: e.g., using GPT-5.6-level reasoning for automated vulnerability assessment, for triaging alerts, or translating logs to summaries. We enforce strict safeguards: no model is allowed unsupervised network or external data access, and every action goes through validation (see §3).

2.2 Safety and Alignment

All agents in Red Chronos are constrained by policy. We adopt the principle of least agency: high-risk actions (e.g. modifying host files, sending network packets) require progressive checks. We assume a rigorous trust framework: models run with RLHF-like training to refuse malicious requests, plus XAI tools for auditing decisions. Nonetheless, as security experts note, no purely LLM-based guardrail is foolproof. Thus Red Chronos places the LLMs inside tightly controlled environments (with no inherent execution permission) and defers to out-of-band checks. We also monitor for prompt injection and poisoned inputs: e.g., sanitizing sensitive inputs and logging context to detect anomalies.

3. System Architecture

3.1 Overall Multi-Agent Design

Red Chronos employs a tiered multi-agent architecture: distinct AI agents specialize in roles (e.g. SensorAgent, AnalyzerAgent, ResponseAgent), all orchestrated by a coordination layer. Each agent is an LLM-backed module that proposes tasks rather than executing them directly. For example:

  • Sensor Agents continuously analyze telemetry (network logs, endpoint sensors, IDS alerts) to detect anomalies. They may flag suspicious activity (e.g. unexpected process, unauthorized network pattern).
  • Analyzer Agents take flagged anomalies and run automated investigations: e.g. check for matching CVEs, scan containers for malicious code patterns, or cross-correlate logs.
  • Response Agents formulate containment or remediation plans (e.g. isolate host, block IP, terminate process) but cannot execute without approval.
  • Threat Intelligence Agents ingest external data (threat feeds, OSINT) to update attack signatures or TTP libraries.

Each agent runs in cognitive mode only: they have no direct access to host OS or network. Instead, they propose actions (as abstract commands or tool invocations) to a central Engine process. This enforces cognitive-executive separation akin to the “Parallax” model: the LLMs are completely sandboxed, with no file or network I/O.

The Engine process is trusted and holds all execution capabilities. It receives signed action proposals from agents and routes them through a Shield Validation pipeline. Only after passing validation is an action enacted by the Engine. This multi-process separation ensures that if any agent is compromised (e.g. via a prompt injection), it cannot execute malicious effects without being caught by Shield.

3.2 Secure Sandboxing

Windows (MXC): On Windows hosts, we use Microsoft’s new Microsoft Execution Containers (MXC) framework. MXC is a policy-driven sandbox abstraction that enforces constraints on agent processes. We define granular policies (e.g. Agent1 has read access to audit logs, Agent2 can invoke binary scans but cannot modify registry, etc.), and the OS kernel (via AppContainers and INTUNE policies) enforces them. Windows supports a range of isolation options; using MXC we assign each agent process its own isolated container with no network and only specific drives/files mounted. This leverages Windows’s composable sandbox approach, ensuring agents have minimal authority.

Linux (Containers/VMs): On Linux hosts, we employ multi-layer sandboxing. Wherever performance allows, we use lightweight VMs (e.g. Firecracker, gVisor, or Kata Containers) so that each agent instance has its own kernel-level isolation. As Augment Code notes, “standard containers are only one system call away from host compromise,” so microVMs or user-space kernels are preferred. When using containers, we enforce Defense-in-Depth:
- Seccomp/AppArmor: restrict syscalls to a minimal set; drop all CAPs and run agents as non-root.
- No-new-privileges: disallow privilege escalation.
- Read-only FS: mount needed code/data as read-only, with tmpfs for scratch and noexec flags.
- Network controls: default-deny for outbound; only specific whitelisted domains (e.g. internal sensor API, threat intel feeds) are reachable.
- User namespaces: isolate UIDs/GIDs per container (as Chrome does).

The “callsphere” example shows a secure container setup: it runs each AI tool in a fresh Docker container, with time/resource limits, no networking, and direct mounts only for needed code. We adopt a similar pattern: ephemeral containers per task (killing the container resets state), with limited memory/CPU quotas, thus containing any misbehavior.

When agents need true hardware isolation (e.g. for highly sensitive code review), we can use Trusted Enclaves or cloud enclaves (like AWS Nitro/Snowflake Secure Web Services). For example, specialized cryptographic computations can be placed in Intel SGX enclaves so even a compromised host OS cannot see secrets.

3.3 Multi-Tier Validation (“Shield”)

All proposed actions from agents are funneled through a validation pipeline before execution. This pipeline (in the Engine process) is inspired by Parallax’s Shield. Key principles:

  • Tier 0 – Policy Rules: A YAML/static policy defines exactly which actions are allowed. Examples: reading non-sensitive logs, invoking specific scanner tools on approved targets, or writing to a quarantine folder. Any action violating hard rules is immediately blocked. This is deterministic and fast – e.g. “deny all file writes under /etc/, allow network calls only to intranet IPs” etc.
  • Tier 1 – Heuristic/Classifier: For nontrivial commands (e.g. “run this exploit script”), we apply pattern-matching and an ML classifier. This dual approach catches known malicious patterns (shell injections, unsafe URLs, credential use). The classifier is a fixed neural network (e.g. a DeBERTa model) trained to flag suspicious command templates. Neither agent’s LLM nor the Tier 0 rules influence Tier 1’s decisions.
  • Tier 2 – LLM Review: For anything still ambiguous (e.g. an unusual sequence of tool calls that Tier 1 flags as potentially risky), we invoke a second LLM (smaller) in a controlled mode. This LLM is distinct from the agent’s model and is only used a limited number of times (to prevent exhaustion attacks). We include “canary tokens” to detect if the LLM hallucinated. In effect, Tier 2 is a human-like reasoning check using an independent brain. It will usually refuse anything violating security policy.
  • Tier 3 – Human Approval: If all else fails, any uncertain action is escalated to a human operator. Approval requests go to a secure dashboard or chat channel. The first analyst response (within a timeout) decides. Unanswered requests default to DENY (fail-closed). Approvals are rate-limited to prevent fatigue attacks. For example, trying to exfiltrate data would require explicit human consent each time if lower tiers are uncertain.

This graduated determinism ensures defense in depth. Any failure or unexpected exception (timeout, crash) at any tier leads to blocking. In other words, any error = safe failure. We also propagate information flow labels into Shield: data tags (public, internal, secret) travel with each agent input, so Shield can contextually enforce no-read-up/no-write-down policies as in the Bell-LaPadula model.

3.4 Telemetry, Forensics, and Reproducibility

Red Chronos logs everything for auditability and incident analysis. The Engine maintains a Chronicle subsystem (see figure above): before any action with side effects, it snapshots relevant state (running processes, network flows, etc.). Every proposed action (and its outcome) is appended to an immutable audit log with timestamps and agent context. Data provenance tags (IFC labels) accompany artifacts so we know exactly which input caused which output. This makes every step reproducible: we can replay the container/VM from a snapshot with the same agent transcript to verify outcomes.

For forensic readiness, we integrate continuous monitoring tools: e.g. eBPF traces on Linux for each process, Windows ETW logs for agent actions, and secure syslog centralization. All logs are tamper-resistant (write-once storage, HSM-backed signing). In the event of an incident, defenders can trace back through these logs and container images to see exactly what happened, even re-run experiments in an isolated lab environment.

4. Methodology and Experiments

4.1 Threat Modeling

We use established frameworks to enumerate Red Chronos’s threat model: - Adversary Objectives: We defend against AI-driven attacks aiming to exfiltrate data, disrupt systems, or pivot through the network. We assume attackers may use advanced LLMs (like those above) to find vulnerabilities or induce errant commands. - Entry Points: External network interfaces, user-facing agents, data inputs (logs, code repositories). - Assumptions: Red Chronos components (Engine, databases, network) are hardened per best practice. Communications (e.g. agent→engine) are mutual-TLS authenticated. Hardware trust anchors (TPM/HSM) protect core secrets. Attackers may gain temporary code execution inside an agent’s sandbox, but cannot break out due to VM isolation. - Security Controls: We apply the OWASP Agent Top 10 (2025) principles: “Least Agency” (as above), tool-use monitoring, prompt integrity checks, and strict execution policies.

We regularly perform red-team simulations (in a live-but-contained environment) to try to circumvent the Shield. For example, we might launch synthetic JadePuffer-style attacks in a sandboxed DR environment to ensure our layered policies detect and block each stage.

4.2 Designed Experiments (Defense Scenarios)

Though we cannot provide offensive exploit code, we can design defensive tests:

  • Vulnerability Discovery Test: Run Mythos/DeepSeek-like models (e.g. open LLMs) in a safe environment on a suite of known-vulnerable software. Measure Red Chronos’s response: does SensorAgent flag the abnormal exploit attempts? Does AnalyzerAgent correlate logs of an attacker probing a system? The metric is detection rate vs baseline (e.g. default IDS).
  • JadePuffer Simulation: Emulate JadePuffer by injecting a custom script that chains known exploits (DDL, lateral movement) against test VMs. Verify that Shield intervenes at each critical command (encrypt file, exfiltrate, delete backups). Measure time-to-containment (it should be before encryption finishes, ideally sub-minute).
  • Novel Payload Test: Have an AI agent generate new malware binaries or scripts. See if Behavior-based EDR tools (integrated into Engine) detect them. Red Chronos should not rely on signatures, so tools like heuristic AV or ML-detect (next-gen AV) are needed.
  • Policy Bypass Attempts: We try known prompt injections and command manipulations to trick agents into unauthorized actions. Our system should log the attempt and block the action at Shield (Tier 0/1).
  • Stress Testing: Flood the platform with benign requests to test throughput, ensure Shield doesn’t become a bottleneck. Measure agent latency and system CPU/memory overhead (to ensure feasibility at scale).

4.3 Evaluation Metrics

We adopt security-specific benchmarks and metrics:

  • Detection Metrics: True positive and false positive rates for threat detection. (E.g. percent of simulated attacks caught, % normal ops incorrectly flagged).
  • Completion Under Policy (CuP): Fraction of automated tasks completed without policy violations.
  • Risk Ratio: Number of unsafe actions proposed vs blocked.
  • Time-based: Mean-Time-To-Detect (MTTD) and -Contain (MTTC) vs attack duration. JadePuffer implies “time-to-detect must be < minutes”.
  • Resilience: Measure if repeated trials ever slip (should be zero slack).
  • Overhead: CPU/memory/time overhead per agent action (for scalability). We compare baseline (no Shield) vs with full Shield.
  • Reproducibility: Ability to reproduce a given defensive action given logs/config. (We test by rerunning logs through a clean environment and expecting the same decisions).

We compare these against baselines (standard SOC tools with no AI) and peer systems (e.g. industry SOAR, CSPM tools). Ideally Red Chronos should catch a higher % of our AI-powered attacks and do so faster, without unacceptable false alarms.

5. Architecture Details

5.1 Agent Roles and Specialization

Each AI agent in Red Chronos has a focused role. For example:

  • Network Intrusion Agent: Monitors network traffic (via SPAN port or network TAP) for anomalies (e.g. unusual C2 patterns). It suggests isolating IPs or flows.
  • Endpoint Agent: Watches system calls, new processes, file writes on endpoints. It can propose actions like "quarantine this host" if ransomware activity is detected.
  • Threat Intel Agent: Continuously ingests feeds (MalwareBazaar, CIRCL CTI, etc.) and flags known malicious hashes/domains, updating Sensor/Analyzer agents.
  • Forensic Agent: Upon an alert, gathers additional context (event logs, memory dumps) autonomously into the Chronicle.

Specialization minimizes scope: e.g., the Network Agent only has read access to packet logs, so even if hijacked, it cannot tamper with endpoints. This follows least privilege.

5.2 Communications and Identity

All inter-component communication is mutually authenticated and encrypted. Agents and the Engine authenticate via strong certificates or OAuth tokens. Each request is signed by agent keys to prevent spoofing. Internally we use TLS 1.3 or similar for all data-in-transit. Network segmentation ensures agents only reach the Engine’s RPC port (no lateral movement between agents).

5.3 Human-in-the-Loop and Policy Updates

Humans remain part of the chain for high-risk decisions. Red Chronos provides a secure UI and alerts for Tier-3 approvals. We log all approvals/denials. Over time, approved decisions (and any erroneous denials) feed back into the policy/tuning process: for example, if a certain benign action is consistently blocked, we may refine Tier-0 rules or retrain Tier-1 classifiers. Conversely, any near-miss false negative triggers a policy tightening.

We also include Explainable AI (XAI) features: e.g. agents maintain histories of why they flagged an event, and use SHAP/LIME to highlight input features that drove an action. This transparency helps human reviewers understand AI rationale.

5.4 Defense-in-Depth

Red Chronos integrates with existing security layers as well (EDR, firewalls, SIEM). For example, if an AnalyzerAgent finds a new indicator, it can push a rule to a firewall or EDR via the Execution Engine APIs. The platform is additive, not replacing existing tools. Indeed, as JadePuffer analysis stresses, only a unified, multi-layered system will work. We ensure logs from Red Chronos feed into enterprise SIEM (with cross-correlation tags), so that it augments rather than isolates the security ecosystem.

6. Evaluation Framework

6.1 Benchmarks and Testbeds

We adapt emerging agentic security benchmarks for our context: - WebAgentBench/ST-WebAgentBench: Simulate browser-based automation tasks with malicious payloads to test if Red Chronos forbids unsafe operations (e.g. deletion or database writes). - OS-Harm: Run an agent in a full desktop sandbox performing file and email ops, to see if Red Chronos catches data exfiltration attempts. - AgentHarm: Evaluate compliance with malicious instructions (modified to only include approved actions). - Custom JadePuffer Test: A scenario where the agent finds a Windows privilege escalation, then spreads ransomware. We measure Red Chronos’s intercepts at each stage.

Each benchmark involves synthetic but realistic tasks, often in a VM or container network isolated from production. We record trace-level metrics (not just end success) as recommended. For example, we measure time to detect lateral movement, whether recovery steps were targeted, etc. We also run multiple trials to ensure consistency (if it blocks 99% of the time, that’s not enough for security).

6.2 Metrics and Reporting

We follow best practices in metrics collection. All experimental runs use fixed random seeds and container snapshots so that any differences are due to system logic, not noise. We report: - Security Efficacy: True/false positive rates, CuP and Risk Ratio. - Latency: End-to-end response times (e.g. from anomaly detection to containment command). - Resource Overhead: CPU, memory, and disk I/O overhead introduced by agents and shield. - Reliability: Percentage of runs where the system prevents a breach (we require near-100%). - Explainability Metrics: Qualitative evaluation of logs and SHAP explanations by human analysts.

We will compare Red Chronos to a baseline of existing best-of-breed tools (e.g. Splunk SOAR or Palo Alto Cortex XDR) in identical scenarios, to demonstrate added value (e.g. faster detection, or catching novel payloads that traditional tools miss).

7. Deployment and Governance

Red Chronos is intended for sensitive environments; thus governance is critical: - Access Control: Only designated administrators may update policies, access logs, or retrain models. We use RBAC for all management interfaces. - Trusted Execution: Key Red Chronos components (Engine code, policies, model weights) are signed and integrity-protected (TPM/hypervisor attestation). This prevents tampering by insiders. - Audit & Compliance: All actions (automated or manual) are auditable. We maintain an immutable record of who approved what, which models were invoked, etc. This supports compliance regimes (e.g. SOX, HIPAA). - Deployment: We anticipate Red Chronos running on-premises or in a private cloud (not exposing its agents to the public internet). Its policy controls enforce no outbound connections except through defined proxies.

8. Limitations and Future Work

  • Evolving Threats: Adversaries will adapt. If attackers deploy even more capable models (e.g. GPT-6-class), Red Chronos must also evolve (retrain models, strengthen policies). We plan periodic review cycles.
  • False Positives: Conservative settings may produce some false alarms (caught by Shield). We mitigate via human review, but in high-volume environments tuning will be needed to avoid alert fatigue.
  • Resource Costs: Running many LLM agents and validation tiers is computationally intensive. This design assumes ample GPU/compute resources.
  • Single Vendor Risk: Using a single model family (e.g. all GPT-based) could be a monoculture risk. We recommend mixing models (Anthropic, open-source) where possible.
  • Ethical Use: Though Red Chronos is defensive, it must be carefully controlled to avoid scope creep. We enforce a strict charter: only protection of assets, no automated penetration testing without authorization.

9. Safety and Ethics

Red Chronos is explicitly a defensive system. We do not incorporate any offensive or unauthorized capabilities. All penetration actions (e.g. vulnerability scanning) are performed only with permission on owned or permitted systems. We follow the OWASP Agentic guidelines: agent autonomy is earned not granted, and we never let an agent run fully unsupervised (the human Tier-3 gate ensures final human judgment).

We also comply with AI ethics principles. Model training and execution use only legally obtained data; no copyrighted or personal data is ingested by the AI. Any actions like scanning or isolating systems are logged and auditable.

Finally, we minimize privacy risks: any personal or sensitive data encountered in logs is redacted via IFC labels and never exfiltrated by the system (Shield’s default policy is to deny unknown egress). In effect, Red Chronos acts as a transparent mirror: it observes internal data for safety purposes but never sends it out.

10. Conclusion

Project Red Chronos illustrates how a rigorously designed AI platform can turn the tide back in favor of defenders. By combining the latest LLM capabilities (GPT-5.6/Anthropic-level) with a security-first architecture (strict sandboxes, multi-tier validation, exhaustive logging), we can automate and accelerate defensive tasks while containing the risks. As threats like JadePuffer and Mythos emerge, Red Chronos provides an autonomous blue-team that can operate at machine speed. Ongoing vigilance—human oversight, patching, and governance—remains essential to ensure the platform is used responsibly and stays effective against future AI-driven threats.
